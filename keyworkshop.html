PART 1: CONCENTRATION UNITS & CONVERSIONS
Q1: Urea solution (0.85% by mass, d = 1.00 g/mL) - Find M, m, χ [15 pts]
FORMULA(s) USED
• Mass % = (mass solute / mass solution) × 100%
• Molarity (M) = mol solute / L solution
• Molality (m) = mol solute / kg solvent
• Mole fraction (χ) = mol component / total mol

✓ ANSWER

Setup: Assume 100.0 g of solution (makes mass % easy)

  • Mass urea = 0.85 g
  • Mass water = 100.0 - 0.85 = 99.15 g

a) Molarity = 0.140 M

mol urea = 0.85 g ÷ 60.62 g/mol = 0.01402 mol Volume = 100.0 g ÷ 1.00 g/mL = 100.0 mL = 0.100 L M = 0.01402 mol ÷ 0.100 L = 0.140 M

b) Molality = 0.141 m

kg solvent = 99.15 g × (1 kg / 1000 g) = 0.09915 kg m = 0.01402 mol ÷ 0.09915 kg = 0.141 m

c) Mole fraction of urea (χurea) = 0.00254

mol water = 99.15 g ÷ 18.02 g/mol = 5.502 mol Total mol = 0.01402 + 5.502 = 5.516 mol χ_urea = 0.01402 ÷ 5.516 = 0.00254 (χ_water = 0.9975)
CONCEPT
  • Molarity uses volume of solution (solute + solvent combined)
  • Molality uses mass of solvent only (not solution!)
  • Mole fraction is dimensionless and all components must sum to 1.000
  • For dilute aqueous solutions where density ≈ 1.00 g/mL, molarity ≈ molality
  • Always pick a convenient basis (100 g for mass %, 1 L for molarity)

Teaching Points / Learning Points

Molarity (M): Temperature-dependent because volume changes with temperature. Used in most lab calculations because we measure volumes easily.
Molality (m): Temperature-independent because mass doesn't change. Preferred for colligative property calculations (ΔTf, ΔTb).
Mole fraction (χ): No units! Used in Raoult's Law for vapor pressure. Think of it as "what fraction of all particles are solute?"
The 100g trick: When given mass %, assume 100 g total so the percentage directly equals grams of solute.
OBJECTIVE: Test ability to interconvert between concentration units using a common reference amount
REMEMBER: "M is for Measuring volume, m is for Mass of solvent" — Molarity has an "r" like "liter," Molality has an "a" like "mass"

Q2: HCl solution (1.00 M, d = 1.05 g/mL) - Find m, χ, mass % [15 pts]
FORMULA(s) USED
• Molarity (M) = mol solute / L solution
• Molality (m) = mol solute / kg solvent
• Mole fraction (χ) = mol component / total mol
• Mass % = (mass solute / mass solution) × 100%
• Density = mass / volume

✓ ANSWER

Setup: Assume 1.000 L of solution (since molarity is given)

Mass of solution = 1000 mL × 1.05 g/mL = 1050 g Moles HCl = 1.00 M × 1.000 L = 1.00 mol Mass HCl = 1.00 mol × 36.46 g/mol = 36.46 g Mass water = 1050 g - 36.46 g = 1013.54 g = 1.01354 kg

a) Molality = 0.987 m

m = 1.00 mol ÷ 1.01354 kg = 0.987 m

b) Mole fraction of HCl (χHCl) = 0.0175

mol water = 1013.54 g ÷ 18.02 g/mol = 56.24 mol Total mol = 1.00 + 56.24 = 57.24 mol χ_HCl = 1.00 ÷ 57.24 = 0.0175

c) Mass percent = 3.47%

Mass % = (36.46 g ÷ 1050 g) × 100% = 3.47%
CONCEPT
  • When starting from molarity, assume 1 L to get moles directly
  • Density connects mass and volume: use it to find total mass
  • Mass of solvent = Mass of solution - Mass of solute (critical step!)
  • The order matters: find what you know → calculate what you need → convert units

Teaching Points / Learning Points

Starting from M: The 1-liter assumption gives you moles immediately (M × 1L = mol)
Density is the bridge: Without density, you cannot convert between M and m because you don't know the total mass
Mass of solvent: This is the #1 error — students forget to subtract solute mass from solution mass. The solution = solute + solvent!
Why M ≈ m here: Notice 0.987 m ≈ 1.00 M. For dilute aqueous solutions, these are similar because 1 L of water ≈ 1 kg
OBJECTIVE: Test reverse conversion from molarity to other units using density as the connecting factor
REMEMBER: "Density is the bridge from M to m" — You need density to go from volume-based (M) to mass-based (m) concentrations

PART 2: SOLUTION PREPARATION & DILUTIONS
Q3: Beer's Law Standards - Dilute 0.50 M to make 10.0 mL standards [10 pts]
FORMULA(s) USED
• M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ (Conservation of moles during dilution)
• Rearranged: V₁ = (M₂ × V₂) / M₁

✓ ANSWER

a) For 0.10 M standard: V₁ = 2.0 mL

V₁ = (0.10 M × 10.0 mL) ÷ 0.50 M = 2.0 mL

b) For 0.20 M standard: V₁ = 4.0 mL

V₁ = (0.20 M × 10.0 mL) ÷ 0.50 M = 4.0 mL

c) For 0.30 M standard: V₁ = 6.0 mL

V₁ = (0.30 M × 10.0 mL) ÷ 0.50 M = 6.0 mL

Lab procedure: Pipette the calculated volume into a 10.0 mL volumetric flask, then dilute to the mark with solvent.

CONCEPT
  • M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ works because moles of solute are conserved (you're just adding solvent)
  • The "1" subscript = concentrated/initial, "2" subscript = dilute/final
  • You're always diluting DOWN (M₂ < M₁), so V₁ < V₂
  • This is the foundation of every serial dilution in analytical chemistry

Teaching Points / Learning Points

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂: This equation says "moles before = moles after." Dilution only adds solvent, never solute.
Pattern recognition: Notice V₁ increases linearly with M₂. At M₂ = 0.50 M, V₁ would equal 10.0 mL (no dilution needed).
Dilution factor: 0.50M → 0.10M is a 5× dilution. Quick check: 2.0 mL × 5 = 10.0 mL ✓
Lab reality: You'd use a micropipette for 2.0 mL (precise), not a graduated cylinder (imprecise).
OBJECTIVE: Test practical application of dilution equation for laboratory standard preparation
REMEMBER: "Moles stay the same, volume goes UP, concentration goes DOWN" — Think of adding water to juice: same amount of flavor, more liquid, less intense taste

Q4: Urea Assay - Convert g/L to M, then dilute [10 pts]
FORMULA(s) USED
• Molarity = (g/L) ÷ (g/mol)
• M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

✓ ANSWER

Step 1: Convert 0.30 g/L to Molarity

M₂ = (0.30 g/L) ÷ (60.62 g/mol) = 0.00495 M = 4.95 × 10⁻³ M

Step 2: Calculate volume needed using M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

V₁ = (M₂ × V₂) ÷ M₁ V₁ = (0.00495 M × 20.0 mL) ÷ 0.50 M V₁ = 0.198 mL ≈ 0.20 mL (or 200 µL)

Procedure: Pipette 0.20 mL (200 µL) of 0.50 M urea into a container, dilute to 20.0 mL total volume.

CONCEPT
  • g/L is a mass concentration; M is a molar concentration. They're related by molar mass.
  • Conversion: g/L ÷ g/mol = mol/L = M (dimensional analysis proves it)
  • This is a two-step problem: unit conversion THEN dilution calculation
  • Small volumes (µL range) require micropipettes for accuracy

Teaching Points / Learning Points

g/L → M conversion: This comes up constantly in clinical chemistry. Learn to do it reflexively: divide by molar mass.
The huge dilution factor: 0.50 M to 0.00495 M is ~100× dilution. That's why V₁ is so small.
Practical consideration: 0.20 mL is only 200 µL — you need a micropipette! This is realistic lab work.
Why the hint helps: The problem could be solved other ways, but converting to M first keeps all units consistent with M₁V₁ = M₂V₂.
OBJECTIVE: Test multi-step problem solving combining unit conversion with dilution calculations
REMEMBER: "g/L ÷ MM = M" — Grams per liter divided by grams per mole gives moles per liter (molarity)

PART 3: VAPOR PRESSURE & RAOULT'S LAW
Q5: Vapor Pressure with Glucose (Non-Volatile Solute) [12 pts]
FORMULA(s) USED
• Raoult's Law: Psolution = χsolvent × P°solvent
• Mole fraction: χ = mol component / total mol

✓ ANSWER

a) Calculate moles:

mol glucose = 25.0 g ÷ 180.16 g/mol = 0.1388 mol mass water = 100.0 mL × 1.00 g/mL = 100.0 g mol water = 100.0 g ÷ 18.02 g/mol = 5.549 mol

b) Calculate mole fraction of water:

Total mol = 0.1388 + 5.549 = 5.688 mol χ_water = 5.549 ÷ 5.688 = 0.9756

c) Calculate vapor pressure (Raoult's Law):

P_solution = χ_water × P°_water P_solution = 0.9756 × 23.8 mmHg = 23.2 mmHg Vapor pressure lowering: ΔP = 23.8 - 23.2 = 0.6 mmHg
CONCEPT
  • Glucose is NON-VOLATILE (doesn't evaporate) — only water contributes to vapor pressure
  • Adding any solute lowers vapor pressure because fewer solvent molecules at surface can escape
  • Raoult's Law: vapor pressure is proportional to mole fraction of solvent
  • This is a colligative property — depends on NUMBER of particles, not their identity

Teaching Points / Learning Points

Why vapor pressure drops: Solute molecules "block" surface spots where solvent could evaporate. Fewer solvent molecules at surface = lower vapor pressure.
Non-volatile solute: Glucose has essentially zero vapor pressure at 25°C, so it contributes nothing to total pressure. Only water evaporates.
χsolvent, not χsolute: Raoult's Law uses the mole fraction of the component that's evaporating (the solvent).
Clinical connection: This is why your sweat evaporates slower when you're dehydrated and have more dissolved solutes.
OBJECTIVE: Test application of Raoult's Law for a solution with one volatile component (solvent only)
REMEMBER: "Solute blocks escape — fewer water molecules can leave" — Picture glucose molecules sitting at the water surface like bouncers at a club, preventing water from leaving

Q6: Vapor Pressure with Methanol + Water (Two Volatile Components) [13 pts]
FORMULA(s) USED
• Raoult's Law for each: PA = χA × P°A
• Dalton's Law: Ptotal = PA + PB
• Mole fraction: χA + χB = 1

✓ ANSWER

a) Calculate moles:

mol methanol = 25.0 g ÷ 32.04 g/mol = 0.7803 mol mol water = 125.0 g ÷ 18.02 g/mol = 6.937 mol

b) Calculate mole fractions:

Total mol = 0.7803 + 6.937 = 7.717 mol χ_methanol = 0.7803 ÷ 7.717 = 0.1011 χ_water = 6.937 ÷ 7.717 = 0.8989 Check: 0.1011 + 0.8989 = 1.000 ✓

c) Calculate partial pressures:

P_methanol = χ_methanol × P°_methanol = 0.1011 × 94 mmHg = 9.50 mmHg P_water = χ_water × P°_water = 0.8989 × 23.8 mmHg = 21.4 mmHg

d) Calculate total vapor pressure:

P_total = P_methanol + P_water = 9.50 + 21.4 = 30.9 mmHg
CONCEPT
  • Both methanol AND water are volatile — both contribute to vapor pressure
  • Apply Raoult's Law to EACH component separately
  • Total pressure = sum of partial pressures (Dalton's Law)
  • Even though χmethanol is small (0.10), it contributes significantly because P°methanol is high (94 mmHg)

Teaching Points / Learning Points

Two volatile components = two Raoult's Law calculations: Each compound evaporates independently based on its own mole fraction and pure vapor pressure.
Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures: Total pressure is additive. The gases don't "know" about each other.
Vapor pressure reflects volatility: Methanol (P° = 94 mmHg) is ~4× more volatile than water (P° = 23.8 mmHg). That's why methanol evaporates faster.
Methanol punches above its weight: Despite being only 10% of the moles, methanol contributes 31% of the total vapor pressure (9.50/30.9) because of its high volatility.
OBJECTIVE: Test application of Raoult's Law and Dalton's Law for a two-component volatile mixture
REMEMBER: "Each component evaporates on its own terms" — Apply Raoult's Law separately, then add. High P° means more volatile, more contribution to total pressure.

PART 4: OSMOTIC PRESSURE
Q7: Osmotic Pressure of MgCl₂ (Electrolyte) [25 pts]
FORMULA(s) USED
• Osmotic pressure: π = iMRT
• Van't Hoff factor: i = number of particles after dissociation
• Molarity: M = mol / L
• Temperature: K = °C + 273
• Conversion: 1 atm = 760 mmHg

✓ ANSWER

a) Van't Hoff factor (i) for MgCl₂:

MgCl₂ → Mg²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ 1 formula unit → 3 particles i = 3

b) Moles of MgCl₂:

mol = 35.0 g ÷ 95.21 g/mol = 0.3676 mol

c) Molarity:

Volume = 450.0 mL = 0.4500 L M = 0.3676 mol ÷ 0.4500 L = 0.817 M

d) Temperature in Kelvin:

T = 25°C + 273 = 298 K

e) Osmotic pressure:

π = iMRT π = 3 × 0.817 mol/L × 0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K) × 298 K π = 60.0 atm

f) Convert to mmHg:

π = 60.0 atm × (760 mmHg / 1 atm) = 45,600 mmHg
CONCEPT
  • Osmotic pressure depends on TOTAL particle concentration, not just formula units
  • The van't Hoff factor (i) accounts for dissociation of electrolytes
  • π = iMRT looks like PV = nRT (ideal gas law) — same R constant!
  • Osmotic pressure can be HUGE for concentrated electrolyte solutions
  • Clinical relevance: IV solutions must be isotonic (~7.4 atm, same as blood)

Teaching Points / Learning Points

Van't Hoff factor (i): This is THE critical concept for electrolytes. MgCl₂ creates 3× more particles than glucose at the same molarity.
Why i matters: Colligative properties depend on particle COUNT. 1 mol MgCl₂ = 3 mol particles. 1 mol glucose = 1 mol particles.
The osmotic pressure equation: π = iMRT is derived from ideal gas behavior. Use R = 0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K) and temperature MUST be in Kelvin.
60 atm is enormous: That's like 60 times atmospheric pressure! This is why osmotic pressure is physiologically important — cells can burst (lyse) or shrivel (crenate) in wrong solutions.
Common Errors to Avoid:
  • Forgetting to multiply by i (the #1 mistake!)
  • Using °C instead of Kelvin
  • Confusing molarity with molality (osmotic pressure uses M, not m)
  • Wrong i value (count ALL particles: MgCl₂ gives 3, not 2)
OBJECTIVE: Test comprehensive application of osmotic pressure equation including van't Hoff factor for strong electrolyte
REMEMBER: "i = ions in solution" — Count EVERY particle: MgCl₂ → 1 Mg²⁺ + 2 Cl⁻ = 3 particles, so i = 3. For non-electrolytes (glucose, urea), i = 1.
